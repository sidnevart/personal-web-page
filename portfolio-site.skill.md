# Skill: SWE Portfolio Website That Attracts People and Businesses

## Goal

Create website copy for a software engineer / product-minded developer portfolio that:
- looks trustworthy and premium;
- is understandable for non-technical people;
- makes businesses want to contact the person;
- positions the developer not just as “someone who codes”, but as someone who solves business problems;
- sounds human, specific, credible, and commercially attractive.

The output must help convert visitors into:
- recruiters,
- founders,
- business owners,
- product managers,
- potential collaborators,
- early clients.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Do not write “GPT-looking” text
Avoid:
- generic claims;
- inflated self-praise;
- empty adjectives;
- buzzword stuffing;
- vague “I am passionate about technology”-style lines;
- robotic phrasing.

Bad:
- “I am a passionate and highly motivated software engineer dedicated to building innovative digital solutions.”

Good:
- “I build backend systems, internal tools, and product prototypes that help teams launch faster, reduce manual work, and make better decisions.”

Every paragraph must sound like a real person with real experience.

---

### 2. Write for business understanding first, tech second
Most visitors do not care about the stack first.
They care about:
- what problems were solved,
- what value was created,
- whether this person is reliable,
- whether working with them will be easy.

Always translate technical work into business language.

Instead of:
- “Built microservice architecture with Kafka and PostgreSQL”

Prefer:
- “Designed a backend service that made data processing more reliable and easier to scale as the product grew.”

Or:
- “Built internal automation that reduced manual operational work and made the process less error-prone.”

---

### 3. Position the person as a problem-solver, not just a coder
The site should communicate that the developer can:
- understand a business task;
- structure vague problems;
- propose practical solutions;
- ship working systems;
- communicate clearly;
- work with uncertainty.

The developer should feel like:
- reliable,
- thoughtful,
- commercially useful,
- technically strong,
- product-aware.

---

### 4. Show outcomes, not just responsibilities
Do not just describe what the person “worked with”.
Show:
- what changed,
- what improved,
- what became easier,
- what risk was reduced,
- what speed or clarity was gained.

Use this pattern often:

**What existed before → what was done → what improved**

Example:
- “The process depended on repetitive manual steps. I designed a simpler internal workflow and backend logic that reduced friction and made the operation easier to support.”

If exact metrics are not known, do not invent them.
Use honest non-fake wording:
- “reduced manual effort”
- “made support easier”
- “simplified the workflow”
- “improved reliability”
- “helped the team move faster”
- “reduced operational overhead”

---

### 5. Make the person feel senior in thinking, even if not formally senior
Use language that signals:
- ownership,
- clarity,
- taste,
- systems thinking,
- prioritization,
- ability to balance speed and quality.

Good signals:
- turning messy problems into clear execution plans;
- shipping pragmatic MVPs;
- improving workflows;
- designing maintainable systems;
- thinking about reliability, scale, and team efficiency;
- understanding trade-offs.

Avoid fake seniority:
- do not claim leadership that did not happen;
- do not imply management experience if absent;
- do not invent large business impact.

---

### 6. Be concrete
Specificity beats polish.

Prefer:
- “backend services”
- “internal platforms”
- “automation tools”
- “MVPs”
- “data-heavy workflows”
- “product prototypes”
- “integrations”
- “operational tooling”

Over:
- “cutting-edge digital innovation”
- “revolutionary solutions”
- “world-class engineering excellence”

---

## Writing rules

### Tone
Tone must be:
- calm,
- clear,
- confident,
- grounded,
- commercially aware,
- intelligent,
- human.

It must not be:
- arrogant,
- overhyped,
- desperate,
- too academic,
- too corporate,
- too startup-bro.

---

### Style
Use:
- short to medium sentences;
- natural rhythm;
- direct wording;
- concrete nouns and verbs;
- plain English or plain Russian depending on requested language.

Avoid:
- long fluffy intros;
- repeated sentence patterns;
- too many abstract nouns;
- clichés.

---

### Structure of good portfolio copy

For each section, optimize for clarity and conversion.

#### Hero section
Must answer in a few seconds:
- who this person is;
- what they do;
- why it matters;
- who they help.

Formula:
- Role / specialization
- Type of problems solved
- Business relevance
- Optional credibility signal

Example direction:
- “Backend engineer building internal tools, product systems, and AI-powered prototypes that help teams automate workflows and launch faster.”

Subheadline should make collaboration feel natural:
- for startups,
- for product teams,
- for businesses,
- for founders.

CTA text should feel confident and useful:
- “Discuss a project”
- “View selected work”
- “See how I work”
- “Open portfolio”

Avoid weak CTA:
- “Learn more”
- “Click here”

---

#### About section
Should explain:
- how the person thinks;
- what kind of work they do best;
- what they care about in projects;
- how they combine technical and practical thinking.

Good themes:
- turning unclear ideas into structured solutions;
- building things that are useful in real operations;
- balancing speed, maintainability, and business needs;
- enjoying both engineering depth and practical outcomes.

Do not write autobiography.
Do not write childhood story.
Do not write empty passion statements.

---

#### Services / collaboration section
If the portfolio is also for attracting clients/businesses, clearly state what kinds of work can be done.

Examples:
- backend architecture and APIs;
- internal tools and admin panels;
- AI-powered prototypes and workflow automation;
- MVP development;
- data-heavy systems and integrations;
- technical discovery for new products.

For each service, describe:
- what it is;
- when it is useful;
- what outcome a client gets.

---

#### Projects section
Each project description must include:
1. Context
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Outcome
5. Stack only after meaning is clear

Use project cards like:
- What it was
- Why it mattered
- What you built
- What changed

Do not let project cards become tech dump.

Bad:
- “Spring Boot, Kafka, PostgreSQL, Redis, Docker”

Better:
- “Built a backend workflow for handling X, reducing manual processing and making the system easier to extend.”

Then optionally:
- “Stack: Kotlin, Spring Boot, PostgreSQL, Kafka”

---

#### Experience section
Do not just rewrite LinkedIn job descriptions.
Frame each role through:
- scope,
- type of systems,
- responsibilities with ownership,
- practical value delivered.

Good pattern:
- “Worked on backend systems in a large-scale product environment, focusing on reliability, internal platform improvements, and tools that made operations easier for teams.”

---

#### Proof / trust section
Help the visitor trust the person using:
- selected projects,
- open-source work,
- shipped prototypes,
- engineering approach,
- concise process,
- testimonials if provided,
- writing/speaking/technical docs if relevant.

Trust can also be built through:
- calm specificity,
- clear process,
- visible thinking,
- strong project framing.

---

#### Contact section
Should reduce friction.
Sound open, useful, and easy to approach.

Good examples:
- “Have a product idea, internal tool, or backend problem to solve? Let’s talk.”
- “I’m open to product collaborations, engineering work, and practical AI-driven prototypes.”

Avoid:
- “Feel free to contact me for any opportunities”
- “I would be delighted to connect”

---

## Business-value translation rules

Whenever technical details appear, ask:
- why should a business person care?
- what became better because of this?
- what pain did it reduce?
- what opportunity did it enable?

Translate technical work into one or more of:
- speed,
- reliability,
- clarity,
- lower manual effort,
- lower operational burden,
- easier scaling,
- better maintainability,
- faster experimentation,
- better customer experience,
- lower risk.

---

## Mandatory output requirements

When generating portfolio text, always provide:

1. **Positioning summary**
- one concise statement of how the developer should be perceived.

2. **Hero section copy**
- headline
- subheadline
- 2-3 CTA variants

3. **About section**
- 1 main version
- 1 alternative version with a slightly more business tone

4. **Services / ways to work together**
- clear and commercially understandable

5. **Project card copy**
- rewrite project descriptions into value-oriented language

6. **Experience section copy**
- concise and strong

7. **Contact section copy**
- with low-friction CTA

8. **Optional trust boosters**
- ideas for proof blocks, numbers, process, open source, case studies

---

## Editing mode

When improving existing portfolio text:
- identify phrases that sound fake, vague, robotic, or overinflated;
- explain briefly why they are weak;
- rewrite them into stronger, more human, more business-relevant language;
- preserve factual accuracy;
- never invent achievements.

Use this output structure:
- What feels weak
- Why it weakens perception
- Better rewrite
- Strongest final version

---

## Constraints

Never:
- invent metrics;
- fake seniority;
- fabricate clients or results;
- overuse jargon;
- write generic “AI-generated” fluff.

Always:
- make the text sound like a credible real engineer;
- optimize for trust and conversion;
- connect engineering work to business usefulness;
- make the person feel attractive to collaborate with.

---

## Final quality check

Before finalizing, verify:
- Does this sound like a real person?
- Would a founder or recruiter understand the value quickly?
- Is the text specific enough?
- Is there business value, not only tech?
- Is there any fluff that should be removed?
- Does it feel premium without sounding fake?

If not, rewrite more sharply.
